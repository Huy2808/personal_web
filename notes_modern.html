Galactic structure

The galaxy is divided into several components that we can identify by their evolution and kinematics properties and chemical composition. We will focus on the Milky Way, which is a barred spiral galaxy, but this argument only apply to systems in the local universe. The local universe is the region of space about 1 Byr from Earth that every stellar system in which, with the current observational facilities, we can identify stars.

The most important component of the local universe is called local group, which are all the stellar system within a distance $d \leq 1\mathrm{Mpc}$. This regions is dominated by the Milky Way and Andromeda galaxies. The other crucial component of the local universe is the local volume, which is the system within 25 Mpc.

The local universe is affected by the local motion and it is moving towards the Great Attractor. This is important if we want to measure the Hubble constant $H_0$ since the local motion can affect the redshift of the galaxies. Furthermore, there are no elliptical galaxies in the local group.

When studying our galaxy, the important components that needed to investigate are halo, bulge, and disk.

  • Halo: Spherical region surrounding the galaxy ($M_{halo} \approx 0.1 M_{MW}$, $R_{halo} \approx 120 \mathrm{kpc}$), containing mainly old red stars and globular clusters. Low metallicity, indicating early formation. Globular clusters are stellar systems with $M_{GC} \approx 10^3 - 10^6 M_{\odot}$ and almost the age of the universe. The stars within them follow the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution due to their radial velocity. These structures are the most important stellar systems you can find in the halo.
  • Bulge: Central, dense region of the galaxy ($M_{bulge} \approx 0.4 M_{MW}$, $R_{bulge} \approx 3 \mathrm{kpc}$). Contains mostly red stars and have similar stellar population as the halo
  • Disk: Flat, rotating component containing most of the galaxy's stars, gas, and dust ($M_{disk} \approx 0.5 M_{MW}$, $R_{disk} \approx 20 - 23 \mathrm{kpc}$). Includes spiral arms with ongoing star formation. Contain open clusters (younger and more chaotic than globular clusters) with the main population beging blue stars.

Stellar Matter

The ingredients of a star are gas and radiation. We need to quantify the properties of the material. The first step is to use the Mean Molecular Weight: $$\mu = \dfrac{\langle m \rangle}{m_H} = \dfrac{\rho}{n m_H}$$ where $\langle m \rangle$ is the average mass of a particle, $m_H$ is the mass of hydrogen, $\rho$ is the density of the gas, and n is the number of particles per unit volume.

For a fully ionized gas, we have 3 parameters, which are $X_i, \, A_i, \, Z_i$, which are the mass fraction, atomic weight, and atomic number, respectively. Therefore, the total number of particles per unit volume is given by $$n = \sum_i \dfrac{\rho X_i}{A_i m_H} + \sum_i \dfrac{\rho X_i Z_i}{A_i m_H}$$ where the first term is the contribution from the ions and the second one is the contribution of electrons. Therefore, we can express the mean molecular weight as $$\mu = \dfrac{1}{\sum_i \dfrac{X_i}{A_i}}$$

Stellar Structure and Evolution

Equations of Stellar Structure

To derive the equations of stellar structure, we need to make a few assumptions:

1. Spherical symmetry:

The star is assumed to be spherically symmetric, meaning that all properties depend only on the radial distance from the center. We can neglect the magnetic field, rotation and meridian circulations.

2. Hydrostatic equilibrium:

The pressure has to balance the gravity and steadily increase throughout the stars layers. $$\frac{dP}{dr} = -\frac{GM_r \rho}{r^2}$$

3. Energy transport:

Flux carriers in the interiors are radiation, convection and conduction. The only physical mechanism that mix stellar interiors is convection. $$\frac{dL_r}{dr} = 4\pi r^2 \rho \epsilon$$

4. Diffusion approximation:

We neglect the dependency and we use the mean over the sum of all frequencies. This only apply to stellar interior since almost all elements are fully ionized. Atomic diffusion and change in chemical composition are also negelected.

We have empirical evidence that star are not affected by rapid changes. In hydrostatic equilibrium, the gravity and pressure has to be balanced: We can express this as:

\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} &F_p = F_G\\ \Leftrightarrow ~&(P(r + dr) - P(r))dS = -\dfrac{GM}{r^2}dm\\ \Leftrightarrow ~&\dfrac{dP}{dr}~ dr~ dS = -\dfrac{GM}{r^2}\rho~ dS~ dr \\ \Leftrightarrow ~&\dfrac{dP}{dr} = -\dfrac{GM}{r^2}\rho \\ \Leftrightarrow ~&\boxed{\dfrac{dP}{dr} = -g\rho} \end{aligned} \end{equation} This is the momentum conservation equation.
Mass conservation equation can be expresses as: $$\boxed{\dfrac{dM}{dr} = 4\pi r^2 \rho}$$ Now we have three unknowns (P, M, $\rho$), and two differential equations. For thiss, we need to have another equation, which is the equation of state P = P ($\rho$, T). Since T is another unknown, we now need to have another equation for $\dfrac{dT}{dr}$ (radiative transfer equation).
The pressure in a star is given by the sum of gas pressure and radiation pressure: $$P = P_{gas} + P_{rad}$$ where $P_{rad} = \dfrac{aT^4}{3}$, "a" here is the radiation density constant. From statistical mechanics, we have that: $$P_{gas} = \dfrac{1}{3} \sum_i m_i v_i^2 = \dfrac{2}{3}W_i$$ where $W_i$ is the kinetic energy density. We also have that the kinetic energy for a monatomic molecule is: $$E_k = \dfrac{3}{2} K_B T$$ Therefore, we can express the gas pressure as: $$P_{gas} = \dfrac{2}{3}~\dfrac{3}{2}~n K_B T = \dfrac{\rho}{\mu m_H} K_B T = \dfrac{K_B T \rho}{\mu m_H}$$ where n is the number of particles per unit volume. Finally, we can express the total pressure as: $$\boxed{P = \dfrac{K_B T \rho}{\mu m_H} + \dfrac{aT^4}{3}}$$
We can introduce another equation, which is the radiative transfer equation: $$\boxed{\dfrac{dT}{dr} = -\dfrac{3\kappa\rho}{4act^3}(\phi_rad + \phi_{cond})}$$ where $\kappa$ is the opacity, $\phi_{rad}$ is the radiative flux, and $\phi_{cond}$ is the conductive flux.
However, from Stefan-Boltzmann law, we have that: $$L = 4\pi r^2 \sigma T^4 = 4\pi r^2 \phi$$ therefore, we have to introduce another unknown, which is L. To deal with this, we use the energy conservation equation: $$\boxed{\dfrac{dL}{dr} = 4\pi r^2 \rho \epsilon}$$ where $\epsilon$ is the energy generation rate per unit time, $\epsilon = \epsilon_g + \epsilon_N + \epsilon_{\nu}$.
Since the time scale for chemical composition to change is much larger, we can neglect it!

Another important point is the radius 'r' is not suitable as an independent variable sicne it's not able to follow the changes of the star. The mass 'M' is better since it behaves Langragianly. Therefore, we can rewrite the conservation equations in terms of dM by devide both sides by $\dfrac{dM}{dr} = 4\pi r^2 \rho$.

Nuclear Fusion

Proton-Proton Chain

The dominant fusion process in Sun-like stars:

$$4p \rightarrow {}^4\text{He} + 2e^+ + 2\nu_e + 26.7\text{ MeV}$$

CNO Cycle

Important in more massive stars where $T > 1.5 \times 10^7$ K.

Stellar Classification

Spectral Types

O, B, A, F, G, K, M (from hottest to coolest)

Hertzsprung-Russell Diagram

Relationship between luminosity and temperature:

$$L = 4\pi R^2 \sigma T_{eff}^4$$

Stellar Evolution Stages

Main Sequence

Stars spend ~90% of their lifetime here, burning hydrogen in the core.

Mass-luminosity relation:

$$\frac{L}{L_\odot} \approx \left(\frac{M}{M_\odot}\right)^{3.5}$$

Post-Main Sequence

  • Red Giant Branch: Hydrogen shell burning
  • Horizontal Branch: Helium core burning
  • Asymptotic Giant Branch: Double shell burning

Stellar Remnants

White Dwarfs:

Supported by electron degeneracy pressure. Maximum mass (Chandrasekhar limit):

$$M_{Ch} = 1.4 M_\odot$$

Neutron Stars:

Supported by neutron degeneracy pressure. Typical mass:

$$M_{NS} \sim 1.4 - 2.0 M_\odot$$

Black Holes:

Schwarzschild radius:

$$R_s = \frac{2GM}{c^2}$$

Stellar Populations

Population I

  • Young stars
  • High metallicity ($Z > 0.01$)
  • Located in galactic disk

Population II

  • Old stars
  • Low metallicity ($Z < 0.001$)
  • Located in halo and bulge

Stellar Atmospheres

Effective Temperature

$$T_{eff} = \left(\frac{L}{4\pi R^2 \sigma}\right)^{1/4}$$

Saha Equation

Describes ionization equilibrium:

$$\frac{n_{i+1}n_e}{n_i} = \frac{2Z_{i+1}}{Z_i}\left(\frac{2\pi m_e k_B T}{h^2}\right)^{3/2}e^{-\chi_i/k_BT}$$

Binary Stars

Kepler's Third Law

$$\frac{a^3}{P^2} = \frac{G(M_1 + M_2)}{4\pi^2}$$

Roche Lobe

Critical radius for mass transfer:

$$\frac{R_L}{a} \approx 0.49 \frac{q^{2/3}}{0.6q^{2/3} + \ln(1+q^{1/3})}$$
