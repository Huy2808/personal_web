Radiative Process

Last updated: October 2025 Astrophysics

Introduction

Astronomy is the science of observation and from the radiation observed, we learned about what is going on in the universe through radiative processes.

Fundamental Equations

The specific intensity is defined as:

$$I_\nu = \frac{dE}{dA \, dt \, d\nu \, d\Omega}$$

where $E$ is energy, $A$ is area, $t$ is time, $\nu$ is frequency, and $\Omega$ is solid angle.

Blackbody Radiation

The Planck function describes the spectral radiance of blackbody radiation:

$$B_\nu(T) = \frac{2h\nu^3}{c^2} \frac{1}{e^{h\nu/k_BT} - 1}$$

where:

  • $h$ is Planck's constant
  • $\nu$ is frequency
  • $c$ is the speed of light
  • $k_B$ is Boltzmann's constant
  • $T$ is temperature

Radiative Transfer

The equation of radiative transfer is:

$$\frac{dI_\nu}{ds} = -\kappa_\nu \rho I_\nu + j_\nu \rho$$

where $\kappa_\nu$ is the absorption coefficient and $j_\nu$ is the emission coefficient.

Key Processes

1. Bremsstrahlung (Free-Free Emission)

Radiation from accelerated charged particles. The emissivity is proportional to:

$$j_\nu^{ff} \propto n_e n_i T^{-1/2} e^{-h\nu/k_BT}$$

2. Synchrotron Radiation

Emission from relativistic electrons spiraling in magnetic fields. Power radiated:

$$P = \frac{4}{3} \sigma_T c \gamma^2 U_B$$

where $\sigma_T$ is the Thomson cross-section, $\gamma$ is the Lorentz factor, and $U_B$ is the magnetic energy density.

3. Compton Scattering

The change in photon energy through scattering:

$$\Delta E = E \frac{4k_BT_e}{m_ec^2}$$

Applications in Astrophysics

  • Stellar atmospheres: Understanding spectral lines and continuum emission
  • Interstellar medium: Heating and cooling processes
  • Active galactic nuclei: High-energy emission mechanisms
  • Cosmic microwave background: Blackbody radiation from early universe
