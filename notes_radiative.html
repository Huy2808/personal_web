Fundamental Equations of Radiative Transfer

Radiative transfer equation and how to solve

We have a quantity that does not depend on the distance to the source, which is the specific intensity $I_{\nu}$ (or brightness)$[erg s^{-1} cm^{-2} str^{-1} Hz^{-1}]$. It is defined as the amount of energy per unit time, per unit solid angle and per unit frequencies that passes through a unit area: $$\boxed{I_{\nu} = \dfrac{dE}{dA\, dt \, d\Omega \, d\nu}}$$ The average intensity (over all directions) is defined as: $$\boxed{J_{\nu} = \dfrac{1}{4 \pi} \int_{\Omega} I_{\nu} \, d\Omega }$$ The bolometric (integrated) intensity $[erg s^{-1} cm^{-2} str^{-1}]$ over all frequencies is defined as: $$\boxed{I = \int_{0}^{\infty} I_{\nu} \, d\nu}$$

Consider a radiation field and construct a small element of area dA and some arbitrary orientation. The differential amount of energy from the small solid angle $d\Omega$ at angle $\theta$ to the normal of dA, passing through dA is: $$\boxed{dE = I_{\nu} \cos\theta \, d\Omega \, dA \, dt \, d\nu = dF_{\nu} \, dA \, dt \, d\nu}$$ The net flux in the direction n is obtained by integrating all over the solid angle: $$\boxed{F_{\nu} = \int_{\Omega} I_{\nu} \cos\theta \, d\Omega}$$ However, for isotropic source, $I_{\nu} \neq I_{\nu} $ and net flux is 0 because the contributions from opposite direction cancel out.

The differential momentum flux (radiation pressure) normal to dA is: $$dP_{\nu} = \dfrac{dF_{\nu} \cos\theta}{c}$$ Therefore, the total momentum flux is: $$\boxed{P_{\nu} = \dfrac{1}{2} \int_{\Omega} I_{\nu} cos^2(\theta) d_{\Omega} }$$

Radiative Energy Density

Given a radiation field $I_{\nu}$ and the engergy density per unit solid angle $u_{\nu}(\Omega)$, the energy contained in a volume is: $$dE = I_{\nu} \, d\Omega \, dA \, dt \, d\nu = u_{\nu}(\Omega) \,dA \,c \,dt \,d\nu $$ with $u_{\nu}(\Omega) = \dfrac{I_{\nu}}{c}$ is called the specific energy density. Integrating over all solid angle gives the mean energy density: $$\boxed{u_{\nu} = \int_{\Omega} u_{\nu} (\Omega) \, d\Omega = \dfrac{1}{c} \int_{\Omega} I_{\nu} \, d\Omega = \dfrac{4 \pi}{c} J_{\nu} }$$
The total radiation density is obtained by integrating over all frequencies: $$\boxed{u = \int_0^{\infty} u_{\nu} d\nu = \dfrac{4 \pi}{c} \int_{0}^{\infty} J_{\nu} d\nu} $$

Radiative Pressure

Consider a isotropic radiation field contained in a box. Due to reflection with the walls, each photon create twice the normal component of momentum: \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} p_{\nu} &= \dfrac{2}{c} \int_0^{\infty} d_{\nu} \int_{\Omega} I_{\nu} cos^2(\theta) d\Omega\\ &= \dfrac{2}{c} \int_0^{\infty} d_{\nu} \int_{\Omega} J_{\nu} cos^2(\theta) d\Omega \quad \quad \text{(isotropic source, $I_\nu = J_\nu$)} \end{aligned} \end{equation} Express $d{\Omega} = \sin\theta \, d\theta \, d\phi$, we have: $$\boxed{p = \dfrac{2}{c} \dfrac{2 \pi}{3} \int_0^{\infty} J_{\nu} d\nu = \dfrac{u}{3}}$$

Note:

The relation $p = \dfrac{u}{3}$ is useful in discussing thermodynamics of blackbody!

Radiative Transfer Equation

Since the specific intensity $I_{\nu}$ is independent of distance, therefore we have: $$\dfrac{I_{\nu}}{ds} = 0$$ which is the radiative transfer equation in free space
If we have the medium, then the radiation will be absorbed or emitted.

EMISSION:

We have the emission coefficient $j_{\nu} \; (erg \, s^{-1} \, cm^{-3}\, str^{-1} \, Hz^{-1})$: $$\boxed{dE = j_{\nu} \,dV\, d \Omega \,dt\, d_{\nu}}$$ We also have $j_{\nu} = \dfrac{P_{\nu}}{4 \pi}$, if the emission is isotropic and the emissivity ($\epsilon_{\nu}$) is $\epsilon_{\nu} = \dfrac{P_{\nu}}{\rho}$, then we have: $$\boxed{dE = \epsilon_{\nu} \,\rho \,dV \,dt \,d\nu\, \dfrac{d\Omega}{4 \pi}}$$
Comparing two energy expressions, we have: \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} dE = dI_\nu \, dA\,dt \, d\nu \, d\Omega &= j_\nu \, dV\, dt \, d\nu \, d\Omega \\ \Rightarrow dI_\nu &= j_\nu ds\\ \Rightarrow \dfrac{dI_\nu}{ds} &= j_\nu \end{aligned} \end{equation} Therefore we have the equation for emission only: $$\boxed{\dfrac{dI_{\nu}}{ds} = j_\nu = \dfrac{\epsilon \rho}{4 \pi}}$$
The solution of the equation is then: $$\boxed{I_{\nu} = I_{\nu}(s_0) \, +\, \int_{s_0}^s j_{\nu}(s') ds' }$$

ABSORPTION:

Similarly, we have the absorption coefficient $\alpha_{\nu} \; (cm^{-1})$, we have that: $$dI_{\nu} = -\alpha_{\nu} \,I_{\nu}\, ds = - n\, \sigma_{\nu} \,I_{\nu} \,ds$$ where $n$ is the number density (number per unit volume) and $\sigma_\nu \; (cm^2)$ is the cross-section. Therefore, the equation for absorption only is: $$\boxed{\dfrac{dI_{\nu}}{ds} = -\alpha_{\nu} I_{\nu} = - n \sigma_{\nu} I_{\nu}}$$ Absorption coefficient can also bewritten as: $$\boxed{\alpha_\nu = \rho \kappa_\nu}$$ with $\kappa_\nu \; (cm^2 \,g^{-1})$ is the mass absorption coefficient or opacity
The solution of the equation is then: $$\boxed{I_{\nu} = I_{\nu}(s_0) + \exp\left(-\int_{s_0}^s \alpha_{\nu}(s') ds' \right)}$$
From both emission and absorption, we have the radiative transfer equation: $$\boxed{\dfrac{dI_{\nu}}{ds} = -\alpha_{\nu} I_{\nu} + j_{\nu}}$$

Optical Depth

Instead of working with the distance 's', it is more convenient to work with the optical depth $\tau_{\nu}$ defined as: $$\boxed{d\tau_{\nu} = \alpha_{\nu}ds}$$ or $$\tau_{\nu} (s) = \int_{s_0}^s \alpha_{\nu}(s') ds'$$ The medium is said to be optically thin if $\tau_{\nu} << 1$ and optically thick if $\tau_{\nu} >> 1$.
The radiative transfer equation can be rewritten as: \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \dfrac{dI_{\nu}}{ds} &= -\alpha_{\nu} I_{\nu} +j_{\nu} \\ \Leftrightarrow \dfrac{dI_{\nu}}{d\tau_{\nu}} &= -I_{\nu} + \dfrac{j_{\nu}}{\alpha_{\nu}}\\ \Leftrightarrow \boxed{\dfrac{dI_{\nu}}{d\tau_{\nu}} = -I_{\nu} + S_{\nu}} \end{aligned} \end{equation} with $S_{\nu} = \dfrac{j_{\nu}}{\alpha_{\nu}}$ is called the source function.

Solution

The solution of the radiative transfer equation in terms of optical depth is: \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \dfrac{d}{d\tau_{\nu}}\left( e^{\tau_{\nu}} I_{\nu}\right) &= e^{\tau_{\nu}} S_{\nu}\\ \Leftrightarrow \dfrac{d\mathcal{I}_{\nu}}{d\tau_{\nu}} &= \mathcal{S}_{\nu} \end{aligned} \end{equation} with $\mathcal{I}_{\nu} = e^{\tau_{\nu}} I_{\nu}$ and $\mathcal{S}_{\nu} = e^{\tau_{\nu}} S_{\nu}$.
Integrating both sides, we have the solution: $$\boxed{\mathcal{I}_{\nu}(\tau_{\nu}) = \mathcal{I}(0) + \int_{0}^{\tau_{\nu}} \mathcal{S}(\tau_{\nu}') d\tau_{\nu}'}$$ In form of terms $I_{\nu}$ and $S_{\nu}$, we have: $$\boxed{I_{\nu}(\tau_{\nu}) = e^{-\tau_{\nu}}I_{\nu}(0) + \int_0^{\tau_{\nu}} e^{\tau_{\nu} - \tau_{\nu}'} S_{\nu}(\tau_{\nu}') d\tau_{\nu}'}$$ We can see that there are two components: the initial intensity attenuated by absorption and the integrated emission from the medium dimishied by absorption

Mean free path

We have that the mean optical depth $\langle \tau_{\nu} \rangle = 1$, however, we also have that: $$\langle \tau_{\nu} \rangle = \int_0^s \alpha_{\nu}(s') \,ds' = \alpha_{\nu} \,l_{\nu} = 1$$ Therefore, the mean free path is: $$\boxed{l_{\nu} = \dfrac{1}{\alpha_{\nu}}}$$

Eddington Limit

Since the radiation carries momentum, it exerts a force on medium, therefore we have the flux vector: $$\boxed{\vec{F_{\nu}} = \int_{\Omega} I_{\nu} \,\hat{n} \, d\Omega}$$ This flux vector is associated with a momentum vector: $$\boxed{\vec{\mathcal{F}} = \dfrac{1}{c} \int_0^{\infty} \alpha_\nu \,\vec{F_{\nu}} \,d\nu}$$ The force per unit mass is then: $$\boxed{\vec{f} = \dfrac{\vec{\mathcal{F}}}{\rho} = \dfrac{1}{c} \int_0^{\infty} \kappa_\nu \,\vec{F_{\nu}} \,d\nu} $$ with $\alpha_{\nu} = \rho \, \kappa_{\nu}$
Let assume a star with mass M and luminosity L and a nearby cloud of distance R with constant opacity $\kappa$. The cloud experiences two forces: $F_g$ and $f_{rad}$. For the cloud to be stable, we need $F_g = f_{rad}$: \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} F_g &= f_{rad}\\ \Leftrightarrow \dfrac{GM}{R^2} &= \dfrac{\kappa F}{c} \\ \Leftrightarrow \dfrac{GM}{R^2} &= \dfrac{\kappa L}{4 \pi R^2 c} \\ \Leftrightarrow \dfrac{M}{L} &= \dfrac{\kappa}{4 \pi G c} \end{aligned} \end{equation} Therefore, we have the Eddington Limit is $\boxed{\dfrac{M}{L} = \dfrac{\kappa}{4 \pi G c}}$. The luminosity sastisfies this relation is called the Eddington Luminosity.

Blackbody Radiation

Blackbody radiation is the radiation which is it self in thermal equilibrium.

Though Experiment:

A blackbody cavity contains radiation $I_{\nu}$ at temperature T. The cavity has small hole to measure the radiation without disturbing the euilibrium. Connect the hole to a filter and another cavity contain radiation $I_{\nu}'$ also at temperature T. If $I_{\nu} \neq I_{\nu}'$, then the radiation will flow between two cavities.

But they have same temperature, therefore it would violate the 2nd law of thermodynamics.

So $I_{\nu} = I_{\nu}'$ and $I_{\nu}$ depends only on the temperature T. Then we have: $$\boxed{I_{\nu} = B_{\nu}(T) = \text{Planck function}}$$
This kind of setup is similar to a medium with very high optical depth.

Kirchoff's Law for thermal emission

Consider a thermal emitter at temperature T in a blackbody cavity at the same temperature. As the optical depth $\tau_{\nu}$ is very high, $I_{\nu} \rightarrow S_{\nu}$ (showed in the solution of radiative transfer equation). Combined with the argument from the blackbody radiation part, we have: $$\boxed{S_{\nu} = \dfrac{j_{\nu}}{\alpha_{\nu}} = B_{\nu}}$$ which is called Kirchoff's Law.
The radiative transfer equation of thermal radiation is then: $$\boxed{\dfrac{dI_{\nu}}{d\tau_{\nu}} = -I_{\nu} + B_{\nu}}$$ from this equation, we can say that thermal radiation ($S_{\nu} = B_{\nu}$) becomes blackbody radiation ($I_{\nu} = B_{\nu}$) only for optically thick medium ($\tau_{\nu} >> 1$).

Thermodynamics of Blackbody radiation

Consider a blackbody cavity with a piston, it has volume V, temperature T, pressure p and energy density u.
From the 1st law of thermodynamics, we have $dQ = dU + pdV$, with Q is heat and U is total energy. From the 2nd law, we have $dS = \dfrac{dQ}{T}$, therefore: \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} dS &= \dfrac{dU + pdV}{T}\\ &= \dfrac{d(uV) + \dfrac{u}{3}dV}{T}\\ & = \dfrac{V}{T} \dfrac{du}{dT} dT + \dfrac{u}{T}dV + \dfrac{1}{3} \dfrac{u}{T} dV\\ &= \dfrac{V}{T} \dfrac{du}{dT} dT + \dfrac{4}{3} \dfrac{u}{T} dV \end{aligned} \end{equation} From this we can see that $S = S (T, V)$, and: $$\left(\dfrac{dS}{dT}\right)_V = \dfrac{V}{T} \dfrac{du}{dT} \quad \left(\dfrac{dS}{dV}\right)_T = \dfrac{4}{3} \dfrac{u}{T}$$ Since $dS$ is a exact differential, we have: $$\dfrac{d^2 S}{dT dV} = \dfrac{1}{T} \dfrac{du}{dT} = \dfrac{4}{3T} \dfrac{du}{dT} - \dfrac{4u}{3T^2}$$ From this relation, we have the differential equation: \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \dfrac{du}{dT} &= \dfrac{4u}{T}\\ \Leftrightarrow \dfrac{du}{u} &= 4 \dfrac{dT}{T} \\ \Leftrightarrow ln(u) &= 4ln(T) + ln(a) \quad \quad \text{('a' is a constant)}\\ \Leftrightarrow u&= a\,T^4 \end{aligned} \end{equation} Thus, we have the Stefan-Boltzmann law: $$\boxed{u(T) = a\,T^4}$$
Since $I_{\nu} = J_{\nu}$ for isotropic radiation, we have: $$u = \dfrac{4 \pi}{c} \int B_{\nu}(T) d_{\nu} = \dfrac{4 \pi}{c} B(T) = aT^4$$ Therefore, the Planck Function is defined as: $$\boxed{B(T) = \int B_{\nu}(T) d\nu = \dfrac{ac}{4 \pi} T^{4} }$$ Since flux from an isotropically emmiting surface is $\pi \times brightness$, we have: $$F = \int F_{\nu} d\nu = \pi \int B_{\nu}(T) d\nu = \pi B(T) = \dfrac{ac}{4} T^4$$ therefore we have another form of of Stefan-Boltzmann law: $$\boxed{F = \sigma T^4}$$ with $\sigma = \dfrac{ac}{4}$ is called the Stefan-Boltzmann constant.
Now we can have the entropy of a blackbody: $$\dfrac{dS}{dV} = \dfrac{4}{3} \dfrac{u}{T} = \dfrac{4a}{3} T^3$$ therefore, the entropy is: $$\boxed{S = \dfrac{4a}{3} T^3 V}$$ This equation gives the 3rd law of thermodynamics as $T \rightarrow 0$, $S \rightarrow 0$.
If S = const, then we have the adiabatic expansion, where: $$TV^{1/3} = \text{const}\quad \text{or} \quad pV^{4/3} = \text{const}$$

Planck Spectrum

Since $B_{\nu} = I_{\nu}$ and $u_{\nu}(\Omega) = \dfrac{I_{\nu}}{c}$, we have that: $$B_{\nu} = u_{\nu} c = \rho_s\, \bar{E} \,c$$ Therefore, to derive $B_{\nu}$, we need to find the density of photon states ($\rho_s$) and the average energy per photon states ($\bar{E}$).
The density of photon states is: $$\boxed{\rho_s = \dfrac{2 \nu^2}{c^3}}$$
The probability of a state with energy $E_n$ is: $$P = e^{-\beta E_n} \quad, \beta = \dfrac{1}{K_B T}$$ Therefore, the average energy per photon state is: $$\bar{E} = \dfrac{\sum_{n = 0}^\infty E_n e^{-\beta E_n}}{\sum_{n = 0}^\infty e^{-\beta E_n}} = \dfrac{d}{d\beta} \ln(\sum_{n = 0}^\infty e^{-\beta E_n})$$ We can rewrite the partition function as: \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} Z &= \sum_{n = 0}^\infty e^{-\beta E_n}\\ \Leftrightarrow Z &= \sum_{n = 0}^\infty e^{- n\,\beta \,h\,\nu} \\ \Leftrightarrow Z &= \dfrac{1}{1 - e^{\beta \,h\,\nu}} \quad \quad \text{(Geometric series)}\\ \end{aligned} \end{equation} Therefore, we have : $$\bar{E} = -\dfrac{d}{d\beta} \ln(\sum_{n = 0}^\infty e^{-\beta E_n}) = -\dfrac{d}{d\beta}(1 - e^{\beta \,h\,\nu})^{-1} = \dfrac{h\,\nu\,e^{\beta\,h\,\nu}}{1- e^{\beta \,h\,\nu}}$$ The average energy is then becomes: $$\boxed{\bar{E} = \dfrac{h\,\nu}{\exp\left(\dfrac{\,h\,\nu}{K_BT}\right) - 1} }$$
Combining both results, we have the Planck function: $$\boxed{B_{\nu} = \rho_s \, \bar{E} \, c = \dfrac{2h \nu^3}{c^2 \left( \exp\left(\dfrac{\,h\,\nu}{K_BT}\right) - 1 \right) }}$$ In terms of wavelength, we have: $$\boxed{B_{\lambda} = \dfrac{2h c^2 \lambda^{-5}}{\left( \exp\left(\dfrac{\,h\,\nu \,c}{\lambda K_B T}\right) - 1 \right) }}$$

Properties of the Planck law

  • Rayleigh-Jeans regime
    In low frequencies ($h\nu << K_B T$), we have: $$\exp\left( \dfrac{h\nu}{K_B T} \right) \approx 1 + \dfrac{h\nu}{K_B T}$$ Therefore, the Planck function reduces to: $$\boxed{B_{\nu} \approx \dfrac{2\nu^3}{c^2} \dfrac{K_B T}{h \nu} = \dfrac{2 \nu^2}{c^2} K_B T}$$
  • Wien regime:
    In high frequencies ($h\nu >> K_B T$) the Planck function reduces to: $$\boxed{B_{\nu} \approx \dfrac{2 h \nu^3}{c^2} \exp \left( -\dfrac{h \nu}{K_B T}\right)}$$ We also want to find the maximum in the blackbody spectrum, which is: $$\dfrac{dB_{\nu}}{d\nu} = 0 \Rightarrow \dfrac{d}{d\nu} \left( \dfrac{2 h \nu^3}{c^2} \dfrac{1}{\exp\left(x\right) -1} \right) = 0 \quad, x = \dfrac{h \nu}{K_B T} $$ solving this equation gives $x \approx 2.82$, therefore we have: $$\boxed{h \nu_{max} = 2.82 K_B T}$$ which is called the Wien's displacement law.
  • Monotonicity with temperature
    Since $\dfrac{dB_{\nu}}{dT} > 0$, therefore no two values of T can give the same value of $B_{\nu}$ at a given frequency $\nu$. More importanly, $\nu$ increase leads to higher T.
  • Brightness Temperature
    The brightness at a certain frequency can be characterized by give the temperature of the blackbody having the same brightness at that frequency. That said: $$\boxed{I_{\nu} = B_{\nu}(T_b)}$$ with $T_b$ is called the brightness temperature.
    It is usually used in radio astronomy, which is in the Rayleigh-Jeans regime. Therefore, $T_b$ can be directly realated to $I_{\nu}$.
    Then, the radiative transfer equation can be rewritten in terms of brightness temperature: $$\boxed{\dfrac{dT_b}{d\tau_{\nu}} = -T_b + T}$$ where T is the temperature of the medium.
    However, brightness temperature is not useful in Wien regiem due to the rapidly decrease of $B_{\nu}$ with $\nu$
  • Color Temperature
    For the source that we dont know exactly the intensity and the distance, we can measure intensity of several frequencies and then fit the data to find the best-fit blackbody spectrum. The temperature optained from the fitted blackbody spectrum is called the color temperature.
  • Effective Temperature
    Total flux F of of the source can be equating to a blackbody that gives the same total flux at temperature $T_{eff}$: $$\boxed{F = \sigma T_{eff}^4}$$ This temperature is called the effective temperature.

Note:

$T_b$ and $T_{eff}$ depend on the magnitude of source intensity while $T_{color}$ depends on the shape of the observed spectrum.

Einstein Coefficients

Deffinition of coefficients

Consider a two-level atomic system with lower energy level 1 and upper energy level 2. The number density of atoms in each level are $n_1$ and $n_2$ respectively. The processes that can occur are:
  • Spontaneous Emission:
    The system is in level 2 and drops to level 1, we then define $$\boxed{A_{21} = \text{the transition probability per unit time for spontaneuous emission}}$$
  • Absorption:
    The system in level 1, then absorb a photon and jumps to level 2. We define $B_{12}$, which gives: $$\boxed{B_{12}\, \bar{J} = \text{transition probability per unit time for absorption}}$$ where $$\bar{J} = \int_{0}^{\infty} J_{\nu} \phi(\nu) d\nu$$ is the mean intensity averaged over a line profile $\phi(\nu)$ since the atom only absorcs photons at certain frequencies.
  • Stimulated Emission:
    The incoming radiation can also trigger the atom in level 2 and drop to level 1, we then define $B_{21}$, which gives: $$\boxed{B_{21} \, \bar{J} = \text{transition probability per unit time for stimulated emission}}$$

Detailed Balance Equations

In thermal equilibrium, we have the absorption rate equal to the total emission rate: $$n_1 B_{12} \bar{J} = n_2 A_{21} + n_2 B_{21} \bar{J}$$ Therefore, we have: $$\bar{J} = \dfrac{A_{21} / B_{21} }{(n_1/n_2) (B_{12}/B_{21}) -1}$$ From statistical mechanics, we have: $$\dfrac{n_1}{n_2} = \dfrac{g_1 \exp(-E/K_B T)}{g_2 \exp(-(E + h\nu_0)/K_B T)} = \dfrac{g_1}{g_2} \exp(h \nu_0/K_B T)$$ where g is the degeneracy coefficient of each level. Therefore, we have: $$\bar{J} = \dfrac{A_{21} / B_{21}}{\dfrac{g_1}{g_2} \dfrac{B_{12}}{B_{21}} \exp(h \nu_0/K_B T) -1}$$ Since in thermal equilibrium of isotropic source, we have $I_\nu = J_\nu = B_\nu$. Since blackbody spectrum is a smooth function, we have $\phi(\nu) \approx \delta(\nu - \nu_0)$, therefore: $$\bar{J} = B_{\nu_0} \Leftrightarrow \dfrac{A_{21} / B_{21}}{\dfrac{g_1}{g_2} \dfrac{B_{12}}{B_{21}} \exp(h \nu_0/K_B T) -1} = \dfrac{2h\nu^3 / c^2}{\exp\left(\dfrac{h \nu_0}{K_B T} \right) -1}$$ From this relation, we can have the Detailed Balance Equations: $$\boxed{\dfrac{A_{21}}{B_{21}} = \dfrac{2 h\nu^3}{c^2} \quad \text{and} \quad \dfrac{g_1}{g_2} \dfrac{B_{12}}{B_{21}} = 1}$$

Note:

If we don't consider the stimulated emission, then mean intensity becomes: $$\bar{J} = \dfrac{g_1}{g_2} \dfrac{A_{21}}{B_{12}} \exp({-h \nu_0 /K_B T})$$ which is similar to Planck function in the Wien regime because at high frequencies $\Leftrightarrow$ level 2 are very far from level 1, thus very high energy states are less populated.

Radiative Transfer Equation with Einstein Coefficients

For emission, we have: $$\boxed{j_{\nu} = \dfrac{h \nu}{4 \pi} n_2 A_{21} \phi_{\nu}}$$ For absorption, we have: $$\alpha_{\nu}^{ab} = \dfrac{h \nu}{4 \pi} n_1 B_{12} \phi_{\nu}$$ For stimulated emission, treating it as negative absroption is more convenient since it is proportional to the intensity and only affects the photon along the beam (analogy to the absorption). Then, we have: $$\alpha_{\nu}^{se} = \dfrac{h \nu}{4 \pi} n_2 B_{21} \phi_{\nu}$$ Therefore, the total absorption coefficient is: $$\boxed{\alpha_{\nu} = \alpha_{\nu}^{ab} - \alpha_{\nu}^{se} = \dfrac{h \nu}{4 \pi}(n_1 B_{12} - n_2 B_{21}) \phi_{\nu}}$$ The radiative transfer equation is then: $$\boxed{\dfrac{dI_{\nu}}{ds} = -\alpha_{\nu} I_{\nu} + j_{\nu} = -\dfrac{h \nu}{4 \pi}(n_1 B_{12} - n_2 B_{21})\phi_{\nu} I_{\nu} + \dfrac{h \nu}{4 \pi} n_2 A_{21} \phi_{\nu}}$$ From this we can have the source function as: $$\boxed{\S_{\nu} = \dfrac{j_\nu}{\alpha_{\nu}} = \dfrac{n_2 A_{21}}{(n_1 B_{12} - n_2 B_{21})} = \dfrac{2 h \nu^3}{c^2} \left( \dfrac{g_2}{g_1} \dfrac{n_1}{n_2} - 1\right)^{-1}}$$ This is the generalisation of the Kirchoff's law. There are 3 cases of this equation:
  • Thermal equilibrium:
    In this cases, we have the relation: $$\dfrac{n_1}{n_2} = \dfrac{g_1}{g_2} \exp\left(\dfrac{h \nu}{K_B T}\right)$$ Replace this into the source function gives $S_{\nu} = B_{\nu}(T)$
  • Non-thermal emission:
    In this cases, $\dfrac{n_1}{n_2} \neq \dfrac{g_1}{g_2} \exp\left(\dfrac{h \nu}{K_B T} \right)$
  • Inverted Population:
    In this cases, $\dfrac{n1}{g1} < \dfrac{n2}{g2}$, which means that the upper levels are more populated, leads to negative $\alpha_{\nu}$. This situation leads to maser and laser phenomena.
    Some astrophysical sources of masers are comets, planetary system, stellar atmospheres, supernovae, AGNs.

    • Scattering

    Pure Scattering

    Consider the scattering is elastic and isotropic, we have the emission coefficent from scattering as: $$j_{\nu} = \sigma_{\nu} J_{\nu}$$ with $\sigma_{\nu}$ is the scattering coefficient (absorption coefficient for scattering). Dividing both sides by scattering coefficient gives: $$S_{\nu} = J_{\nu} = \dfrac{1}{4 \pi} \int I_{\nu} d\Omega$$ Therefore, the radiative transfer equation for pure scattering is: $$\boxed{\dfrac{I_{\nu}}{ds} = -\sigma_{\nu}(I_{\nu} - J_{\nu})}$$ However, it is more convenient to describe the scattering as random walk. The average displacement is $$\langle R \rangle = \langle r_1 + r_2 + r_3 + ... \rangle = 0$$ We also have the mean square displacement as: $$l_{\star}^2 = \langle R \rangle^2 = \langle r_1 \rangle^2 + \langle r_2 \rangle^2 + ... + \langle r_n \rangle^2 = N \,l^2$$ with $l$ is the mean free path of photon and N is the number of scatterings. Therefore, we have: $$\boxed{l_{\star} = \sqrt{N}l}$$
    Consider different medium:
    • Optically thin
      We have the survival probability in a optical depth is $P = e^{-\tau}$. The mean number of scatterings is then: $$\boxed{N = 1 - e^{-\tau} \approx \tau \quad (\text{Taylor expansion})}$$
    • Optically thick
      The size of medium $L$ equal to the mean square displacement. Therefore, $$N = \dfrac{l_{\star}^2}{l^2} = \dfrac{L^2}{l^2} = L^2 \alpha_{\nu}^2 = L^2 \sigma_{\nu}^2$$ From optical depth definition, $\tau = L \alpha_{\nu} = L \sigma_{\nu}$, therefore: $$\boxed{N = \tau^2}$$
    Therefore, we have that $\boxed{N \approx \tau + \tau^2}$ for any optical thickness.

    Combined Absorption and Scattering

    Consider the scattering, the radiative transfer equation becomes: $$\dfrac{d I_{\nu}}{ds} = -\alpha_{\nu}(I_{\nu} - B_{\nu} - \sigma_{\nu}(I_{\nu} - J_{\nu}))$$ Since $B_{\nu} = J_{\nu} = S_{\nu}$, we have: $$\boxed{\dfrac{dI_{\nu}}{ds} = -(\alpha_{\nu} + \sigma_{\nu})(I_{\nu} - S_{\nu})}$$ with $(\alpha_{\nu} + \sigma_{\nu})$ is the called extinction coefficient and optical depth can be redefined as: $$\boxed{d\tau_{\nu} = (\alpha_{\nu} + \sigma_{\nu}) ds}$$ The source function is then: $$\boxed{S_{\nu} = \dfrac{\alpha_{\nu} B_{\nu} + \sigma_{\nu} J_{\nu}}{\alpha_{\nu} + \sigma_{\nu}}}$$ Consider 2 limiting cases:
    • Deep inside a medium with T and $\tau >> 1$
      In thermal equilibrium and optically thick medium, we have $J_{\nu} = B_{\nu}$ and therefore $S_{\nu} = B_{\nu}$
    • Isolated in free space
      In this case, $J_{\nu} =0$, therefore: $$S_{\nu} = \dfrac{\alpha_{\nu}}{\alpha_{\nu} + \sigma_{\nu}} B_{\nu}$$

    Consider the random walk, the mean free path is now: $$\boxed{l_{\nu} = \dfrac{1}{\alpha_{\nu} + \sigma_{\nu}}}$$ During the path, the probability that a free path ends with absorptionis: $$\epsilon_{\nu} = 1 - e^{\tau} \approx \tau = \alpha_{\nu} l_{\nu} = \dfrac{\alpha_{\nu}}{\alpha_{\nu} + \sigma_{\nu}}$$ The probability that a free path ends with scattering is then: $$1 - \epsilon_{\nu} = \dfrac{\sigma_{\nu}}{\alpha_{\nu} + \sigma_{\nu}}$$. Therefore, the source function can be rewritten as: $$\boxed{S_{\nu} = \epsilon_{\nu} B_{\nu} + (1-\epsilon_{\nu}) J_{\nu}}$$ The mean square displacement is $l_{\star} = \sqrt{N}l_{\nu}$, with N is the number of scattering (or number of free paths), then $N = \epsilon_{\nu}^{-1}$. Therefore we have: $$\boxed{l_{\star} = \sqrt{\dfrac{\alpha_{\nu} + \sigma_{\nu}}{\alpha_{\nu}}} \dfrac{1}{\alpha_{\nu} + \sigma_{\nu}} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_{\nu}(\alpha_\nu + \sigma_{\nu})}}}$$ This is also called the diffusion length.
    Apply in the case of a medium with finite size L, we can use an effective optical depth defined as: $$\boxed{\tau_{\star} = \dfrac{L}{l_{\star}}}$$ There are two limiting cases:
    • If $\tau_{\star} << 1$
      The medium is effectively optically thin, therefore radiation can escape the medium before being absorbed. The luminosity is then: $$\mathcal{L}_{\nu} = 4 \pi \alpha_{\nu} B_{\nu} V$$
    • If $\tau_{\star} >> 1$
      The medium is effectively optically thick, therefore radiation is absorbed before escaping the medium. The physical condition at this scale approaches thermal equilibrium. The luminosity is then: $$\mathcal{L}_{\nu} = 4 \pi \alpha_{\nu} B_{\nu} A l_{\star} = 4 \pi \alpha_{\nu} B_{\nu} A \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_{\nu}(\alpha_{\nu} + \sigma_{\nu})}} = 4 \pi B_{\nu}A \sqrt{\dfrac{\alpha_{\nu}}{\alpha_{\nu} + \sigma_{\nu}}}$$ when there's no scattering ($\sigma_{\nu} = 0$), the luminosity reduces to: $$\mathcal{L}_{\nu} = 4 \pi B_{\nu} A$$ which is the luminosity from a blackbody surface of area A.

    Radiative Diffusion

    Rosseland Approximation

    Consider a medium with properties change with depth of the medium. THe intensity depend only on a sigle angle $\theta$, let $\mu = \cos \theta$. The Rosseland mean absorption coefficient is: $$\boxed{\dfrac{1}{\alpha_{R}} = \dfrac{\int_0^{\infty} (\alpha_{\nu} + \sigma_{\nu})^{-1} \dfrac{dB_{\nu}}{dT} d\nu}{\int_0^{\infty} \dfrac{dB_{\nu}}{dT} d\nu}}$$ Then we have the radiative flux as: $$\boxed{F(z) = -\dfrac{16 \sigma T^3}{3 \alpha_R} \dfrac{dT}{dz}}$$ This equation is called the Rosseland approximation.

    Fundamentals of Radiation Fields

    Maxwell's Equations

    First, we have the Maxwell's equations: \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \nabla \cdot \mathbf{D} &= 4 \pi \rho \quad \quad \quad \quad &\nabla \cdot \mathbf{H} = 0\\ \nabla \times \mathbf{D} &= -\dfrac{1}{c} \dfrac{\partial \mathbf{H}}{\partial t} \quad \quad \quad &\nabla \times \mathbf{H} = \dfrac{4 \pi}{c} \mathbf{j} + \dfrac{1}{c} \dfrac{\partial \mathbf{D}}{\partial t} \end{aligned} \end{equation} where $\rho$ and $j$ is the charge and current density respectively. The fields $D$ and $H$ are related to $E$ and $B$ by: \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \mathbf{D} &=\epsilon \mathbf{E}\\ \mathbf{H} &= \dfrac{\mathbf{B}}{\mu} \end{aligned} \end{equation} where $\epsilon$ and $\mu$ are the dielectric constant and magnetic permeability of the medium.
    A consequence of this equation is the conservation of charge: $$\boxed{\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \ H) = \nabla \cdot \mathbf{j} + \dfrac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} = 0}$$

    Poynting Theorem

    We first have the work done by the electromagnetic field per unit volume on a charge distribution from 4th Maxwell's equation: $$\boxed{\mathbf{j \cdot E} = \dfrac{1}{4 \pi} \left[ c(\nabla \times \mathbf{H})\cdot \mathbf{E} - \mathbf{E} \cdot \dfrac{\partial \mathbf{D}}{\partial t} \right]}$$ Using the vector identity: $$\mathbf{E}\cdot(\nabla \times \mathbf{H}) = \mathbf{H}\cdot(\nabla \times \mathbf{E}) - \nabla \cdot(\mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{H})$$ for the equation above, we have: \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} &\mathbf{j \cdot E} = \dfrac{1}{4 \pi} \left[ -\mathbf{H}\dfrac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} - c\nabla \cdot(\mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{H}) -\mathbf{E}\dfrac{\partial \mathbf{D}}{\partial t} \right] \\ &\mathbf{j \cdot E} + \dfrac{1}{8 \pi} \left( \epsilon E^2 + \dfrac{B^2}{\mu^2} \right) = -\dfrac{c}{4 \pi}\nabla(\mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{H}) \quad \quad (\mu \,\text{and}\, \epsilon \text{ are time independent}) \end{aligned} \end{equation} This relation is called the Poynting theorem. This relation can be intepreted as The rate of change of electric field energy plus The rate of change of mechanical energy per unit volume equals to the negative divergence of the field energy flux.
    The term $\boxed{S = \dfrac{c}{4 \pi} \mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{H}}$ is called the Poynting vector, which represents the energy flux (energy per unit area per unit time) of the electromagnetic field. Integrating over a volume element, we have: \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \int_{V} \mathbf{j \cdot E} dV + \dfrac{d}{dt} \dfrac{1}{8 \pi}\int_V \left( \epsilon E^2 + \dfrac{B^2}{\mu}\right) &= -\int_A \mathbf{S \cdot dA}\\ \Leftrightarrow \dfrac{d}{dt}(U_{kin} + U_{field}) & = -\int_A \mathbf{S \cdot dA} \end{aligned} \end{equation} That is, the rate of change of total energy (kinetic + field) within a volume V is equal to the net inward flow of energy through a surface A enclosing the volume.
    If we consider a static system, E and B will be given by Coulomb's law and $E, \, B \propto r^{-2}$. Considering a spherical surface of radius r, the Poynting vector is then $|S| \propto |E| \cdot |B| \propto r^{-4}$. Thus as $r \rightarrow \infty$, $\int_{A} \mathbf{S \cdot dA} \rightarrow 0$. It means that no electromagnetic energy is transported over large distances in a static system.
    However, if we consider time-varying fields, $E,\, B \propto r^{-1}$ (bcz of the retared time), therefore as $r \rightarrow \infty$, $\int_{A} \mathbf{S \cdot dA} \rightarrow \text{finite value}$.

    Electromagnetic Waves

    Consider the Maxwell's equations in vacuum, we'll have $\epsilon = \mu = 1$ With the derivation, we have the solution for E and B fields as: $$\boxed{\mathbf{E} = \hat{\alpha}_1E_0 e^{i(\mathbf{k} \cdot r -\omega t)} \quad, \quad \mathbf{B} = \hat{\alpha}_2B_0 e^{i(\mathbf{k} \cdot r -\omega t)}} $$ with $\mathbf{\hat{a}_1}\, \text{and} \, \mathbf{\hat{a}_2}$ are unit vectors, $E_0, \,B_0$ are the complex constants. The terms $\mathbf{k} = k\mathbf{n}$ and $\omega$ are wave vector and angular frequency respectively.
    Substituting the solution to the Maxwell's equations, we have: \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} &i\mathbf{k \cdot \hat{a}_1}E_0 = 0 \quad \quad &i\mathbf{k \cdot \hat{a}_2}B_0 = 0\\ &i\mathbf{k \times \hat{\alpha}_1} E_0 =\dfrac{i\omega}{c}\mathbf{\hat{a}_2} B_0 \quad \quad &i\mathbf{k \times \hat{a}_2} B_0 = -\dfrac{i \omega}{c}\mathbf{\hat{a}_1}E_0 \end{aligned} \end{equation} From this relations, we have that $\mathbf{\hat{a}_1 \perp \hat{a}_2 \perp k}$, we also have that: $$E_0 = \dfrac{\omega}{k c} B_0 \quad \text{and} \quad B_0 = \dfrac{\omega}{k c}E_0 $$ This implies that $E_0 = B_0$ and $\omega = ck$. This means that a EM wave propagate in vacuum with a phase velocity: $$\boxed{v_{phase} = \dfrac{\omega}{k} = c}$$

    Since E and B and oscillating quantities and have the form of $E(t) = E_0 e^{i\omega t}$ and $B(t) = B_0 e^{i\omega t}$. We have that $$Re(E) = \dfrac{E_0 e^{i\omega t} + E_0^{\star}e^{-i \omega t}}{2}$$ Same for B field. Therefore, the time-averaged Poynting vector is: \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \langle \mathbf{S} \rangle &= \langle \dfrac{c}{4 \pi} Re(E) Re(B)\rangle \\ &= \dfrac{c}{4 \pi} \dfrac{1}{4} (E_0 e^{i\omega t} + E^{\star}_0 e^{-i \omega t})(B_0 e^{i \omega t} +B_0^{star} e^{-i \omega t})\\ &= \dfrac{c}{16 \pi} (E_0 B_0 e^{2i\omega t} + E_0 B_0^{\star} + E_0^\star B_0 + E_0^\star B_0^\star e^{-2i\omega t} )\\ &= \dfrac{c}{16 \pi} (E_0 B_0^{\star} + E_0^\star B_0) \quad \quad (\text{since}\, \langle e^{\pm 2 i\omega t} \rangle = 0)\\ &= \dfrac{c}{8 \pi} Re(E_0 B_0^{\star}) = \dfrac{c}{8 \pi} Re(E_0^{\star} B_0) \end{aligned} \end{equation} Since $E_0 = B_0$, we have that: $$\boxed{\langle \mathbf{S} \rangle = \dfrac{c}{8 \pi} |E_0|^2 = \dfrac{c}{8 \pi} |B_0|^2 }$$ We can also compute the time average of field energy as: $$\boxed{\langle U_{field} \rangle = \langle\dfrac{1}{8 \pi}(E^2 + B^2) \rangle = \dfrac{1}{8 \pi} \dfrac{1}{2} (E_0 B_0^\star + E_0^\star B_0) = \dfrac{1}{8 \pi} |E_0|^2 = \dfrac{1}{8 \pi} |B_0|^2}$$

    Radiation Spectrum

    We can express $E(t)$ in terms of Fourrier transform as: $$\boxed{\hat{E}(\omega) = \dfrac{1}{2 \pi} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} E(t) e^{i\omega t} dt}$$ and the inverse transform is: $$\boxed{E_(t) = \dfrac{1}{2 \pi} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \hat{E}(\omega) e^{-i\omega t}d\omega}$$ therefore we have that: $$\boxed{E^\star(t) = E(t) \quad, \quad \hat{E}(-\omega) = \hat{E}^\star(\omega)}$$ We also have that the instantaneous Poynting vector is $S = \dfrac{d U_{total}}{dt dA} = \dfrac{c}{4 \pi}E^2(t)$
    Therefore, the total power per unit area is: $$\dfrac{dU_{total}}{dt} = \dfrac{c}{4 \pi} \int_{-\infty}^\infty E^2(t)dt = \dfrac{c}{4 \pi} 4\pi \int_0^{\infty} |\hat{E}(\omega)|^2 d\omega = c \int_0^{\infty} |\hat{E}(\omega)|^2 d\omega$$ (from Parseval theorem and $|\hat{E}(-\omega)|^2 = |\hat{E}(\omega)|^2$)
    Therefore, we obtain the spectral energy density per unit angular frequency as: $$\boxed{\dfrac{dU_{total}}{dA d\omega} = c|\hat{E}(\omega)|^2}$$ This is also called the radiation spectrum.

    Note:

    From the uncertainty principle, we have that $\Delta t \Delta \omega \geq 1$, therefore to have a good frequency resolution $\Delta \omega$, we need to observe the signal for a long time $\Delta t$.
