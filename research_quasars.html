Quasars Proper Motion Dipole

Overview

When the observer is moving, it cause the displacement in the apparent positions of celestial bodies in the direction of the velocity. This effect is called aberration. If the velocity changes with time, which is correspond to the acceleration, the change of displacements create a pattern of proper motions in the direction of acceleration. This effect is called secular aberration drift and can be used to detect the acceleration of the Solar System with respect to the rest frame of distant extragalactic sources.

Theoretically, the proper motion field induced by the acceleration of the Solar System has a unique signature in its power spectrum, which is a pure E-mode dipole pattern. However, from the power spectrum in Figure 1, we saw that the higher-order multipoles are non zero, which indicates the presence of systematics in the data or (less likely) the inhomogeineity and anisotropy of the Universe at large scales. To better understand and quantify these effects, we employ simulation-based inference (SBI) techniques to model the proper motion field and extract constraints on the Solar System's acceleration as well as the power spectrum to identify the systematics.

Our model include main components contibute to the small modes in the power spectrum including the secular aberration drift, the small-scale systematics from the larger modes and random shot noise. By simulating many realizations of the proper motion field with different parameters, we can build a dataset to train neural density estimators (NDEs) to learn the posterior distribution of the parameters given the observed summary statistics. This approach allows us to infer the parameters of interest while accounting for the complex noise and systematics present in the data without the need of explicit likelihood of the noise and systematics.

The result in Figure 2 shows the posterior distribution of the Solar System acceleration components along the three axes in equatorial coordinates. The error bars are larger if we include the small-scale systematics in the model.

However, the maximum posterior power spectrum not really match the observed one in Figure 1, indicating that our model for small-scale systematics might not be sufficient. In the future, we will trying to include the effect from the anisotropic expansion from the LTB void model to see if it can better explain the observed power spectrum.
